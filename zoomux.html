A UX Analysis of Zoom

On the surface level of things, Zoom has done a great job at creating a communication tool that can cater to everyone.




With such a large and wide variety of people relying on Zoom, it was no easy task to design a polished user experience that could also satisfy everyone’s functional needs. On the surface level of things, Zoom has done a great job at creating a communication tool that can cater to everyone.


The Good

The home page of the app has a very clean user interface design that provides clear visibility of functionality and system status. The home page is not cluttered by unnecessary buttons and allows users to easily join and schedule meetings through a set of four buttons. The simple navigation bar at the top allows users to access chat logs, meeting recordings and contacts without unnecessary confusion. Another way that Zoom works to seek universal usability is by providing basic and advanced categories for settings pages that could potentially confuse novice users. This allows the app to cater to all types of users without hindering the advanced users access to important settings and functionality.

One way Zoom has worked to improve the user experience of the product outside of the general user interface would be in terms of how Zoom provides users with virtual backgrounds and appearance touch ups. Users are also not put on the spot for having their camera on in Zoom, as the app cycles through participants and only shows those who are currently speaking. Providing such functionality directly improves the user experience because it makes people feel less conscious about their environment and looks. Users are not as confident in interacting online, and giving people quality of life features helps boost their willingness to show themselves and speak on camera.



The Bad


Unfortunately, as the certain aspects of Zoom’s functionality gets more intricate, the user experience starts to decline. The first example of this can be seen in regards to how functionality for polling and breakout rooms is split between the website and the desktop client. For example, users have to upload a csv file on the website in order to get the client to remember the predetermined members of each group. Similarly, poll questions and results cannot be easily set and viewed through the client and require using the webpage. To make the matter worse, changing settings through the webpage can be even more confusing as there is no consistency between how the settings page looks like on the standard client. Another example would be how many panels for viewing chat logs and participants have pop out functionality that is hidden in a drop down menu rather than being fluid drag and drop interactions. Similarly, it can be difficult to figure out how to stop sharing certain sections of the screen. The reason this negatively impacts the user experience is because the user can lose track of their cluttered windows and the system provides no way to permit easy reversal of change.


Conclusion


In summary, I believe it is not easy to label Zoom as just a good or bad example of HCI and user experience. Considering the abnormal amount of load being put on Zoom, I feel they have done a wonderful job of providing the world with a platform to work and collaborate on regardless of location. With that being said however, it seems like Zoom could have paid more attention towards keeping their complex functionality easy to use and learn. All in all I would say that Zoom is still a relatively new product, and that they have a long road ahead of them in terms of perfecting their user experience.
